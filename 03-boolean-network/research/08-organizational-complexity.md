# Organizational Network Complexity

## Kauffman's Organizational Analogy
Organizations can be modeled as Boolean networks where:
- Nodes = decisions, tasks, or business units
- Edges = interdependencies between decisions
- State = current configuration of all decisions
- Fitness = organizational performance

## Key Insights
- **Low K (low interdependence)**: Easy to optimize locally, but limited overall capability
- **High K (high interdependence)**: Rich potential but near-impossible to optimize (complexity catastrophe)
- **Optimal K**: Balance between capability and adaptability

## Hierarchy as a Solution
Hierarchical structures reduce effective K by:
- Creating modular decomposition of decisions
- Limiting cross-module interdependencies
- Allowing local optimization within modules
- Coordinating between modules at higher levels

## Edge of Chaos in Organizations
Organizations at the edge of chaos:
- Enough structure for coordination
- Enough flexibility for adaptation
- Not so rigid they can't respond to change
- Not so chaotic they can't execute

## Patch Procedure for Organizations
Kauffman's patch procedure:
1. Divide organization into semi-autonomous patches
2. Each patch optimizes its own decisions
3. Cross-patch interactions are limited
4. Patches co-evolve, finding good global solutions
5. Analogous to divisional organizational structure

## Implications for Design
- Modular architectures reduce complexity catastrophe risk
- Too much modularity sacrifices synergies
- The right patch size depends on the underlying interdependency structure
- Networks should be "nearly decomposable" (Simon)
