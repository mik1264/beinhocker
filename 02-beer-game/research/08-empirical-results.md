# Beer Game: Empirical Results with MBA Students

## Typical Game Outcomes

### Cost Performance
- **Average team costs**: ~$2,000 (sometimes exceeding $10,000)
- **Optimal performance**: ~$200
- **Performance gap**: roughly **10x worse than optimal**
- Cost penalty: $0.50/case/week holding, $1.00/case/week backlog

### Oscillation Characteristics
- Orders and inventories dominated by **large amplitude fluctuations**
- Average oscillation period: **~20 weeks**
- Inventory peaks average **~40 cases** despite constant demand of 4→8
- Peak factory orders average **more than double** peak retail orders

### Amplification Pattern
Moving upstream through the supply chain:
1. **Customer demand**: Step from 4 to 8 cases/week (then constant)
2. **Retailer orders**: Moderate oscillation
3. **Wholesaler orders**: Larger oscillation
4. **Distributor orders**: Even larger oscillation
5. **Factory orders**: Extreme oscillation (often 2-3x customer demand)

### Phase Lag
- Order peaks occur **progressively later** moving up the chain
- Each echelon's response is delayed by pipeline delays
- Creates appearance of demand waves propagating upstream

## Behavioral Patterns

### Common Player Mistakes
1. **Over-ordering** when backlogs appear (panic response)
2. **Failure to account for pipeline orders** already placed
3. **Misattribution**: Players blame "fluctuating customer demand" for oscillations
4. **Under-ordering** when inventory finally arrives (overcorrection)

### The "Aha Moment"
- When demand pattern is revealed (simple step from 4→8), players are shocked
- They realize their own ordering behavior created the wild oscillations
- Demonstrates that **system structure, not external shocks**, drives dynamics

## Consistent Across Demographics
- Results replicate across MBA students, executives, engineers, and other populations
- Cultural and educational background has minimal effect on outcomes
- The structure of the system overwhelms individual capabilities
