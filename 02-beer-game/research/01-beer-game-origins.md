# Beer Distribution Game: Origins and History

## Creation
- Invented by **Jay Wright Forrester** at MIT Sloan School of Management in **1960**
- Originally developed in the late 1950s as part of Forrester's work on Industrial Dynamics
- Based on Forrester's observations of industrial supply chain behavior
- First published in Forrester's seminal work *Industrial Dynamics* (1961)

## Game Structure
- Four-stage linear supply chain: **Retailer → Wholesaler → Distributor → Factory (Brewery)**
- Each player manages one echelon independently
- Pennies (or poker chips) represent cases of beer
- Card deck represents customer demand
- Originally played with pen, paper, printed plastic tablecloths, and poker chips

## Rules
- Players place orders weekly from their supplier
- Information is **strictly local**: players know only their own inventory, backlogs, and orders placed
- No inter-player communication allowed
- Shipping and order processing delays exist at each stage
- Initial equilibrium: 12 cases inventory, 4 cases/week throughput
- Game typically runs 36-50 weeks

## Purpose
- Demonstrates how simple supply chains can generate complex, oscillating behavior
- Illustrates the **bullwhip effect** (also called the Forrester Effect)
- Shows how individual rational decisions can lead to system-level dysfunction
- Used worldwide in MBA programs, operations management courses, and corporate training

## Key Learning Objectives
1. Information delays cause overreaction and oscillation
2. Local optimization leads to global suboptimality
3. The structure of the system, not individual decisions, drives poor performance
4. Information sharing and coordination are critical for supply chain efficiency
