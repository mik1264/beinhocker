Sugarscape

Growing Artificial Societies — Epstein & Axtell (1996) / Beinhocker Ch. 5

Start with a landscape of "sugar" — a renewable resource distributed in two mountain peaks. Drop hundreds of agents onto this world, each with slightly different abilities: some can see farther, some burn less energy. Give them one simple rule: look around, move to the richest empty spot you can see, eat. That's it. No exploitation, no institutions, no cunning. Yet within a few dozen ticks, the egalitarian utopia collapses into staggering inequality. This is the profound lesson of Sugarscape: inequality is not a bug — it is an emergent property of heterogeneous agents on an uneven landscape.

Beinhocker's Key Insight

In The Origin of Wealth, Beinhocker uses Sugarscape to demolish a common misconception: that inequality requires exploitation, corruption, or unfair institutions. Even in a world of perfectly simple, identical rules — where no agent can cheat, steal, or collude — heterogeneity alone produces inequality. The lesson: inequality is the default emergent state of any system with heterogeneous agents and uneven resources. Reducing it requires deliberate, sustained intervention.

Fun Facts About Sugarscape

  • > Published in 1996, Sugarscape was one of the first large-scale agent-based models. Epstein and Axtell ran it on hardware with less computing power than a modern smartwatch.
  • > The "two mountains" landscape was inspired by geographic resource distribution. The agents' migration toward the peaks mirrors real human settlement patterns.
  • > Adding just two rules — trade and reproduction — produces emergent phenomena including market prices, cultural transmission, warfare, disease epidemics, and economic cycles.
  • > The Gini coefficient in a standard Sugarscape run typically stabilizes between 0.4 and 0.6, comparable to many real-world economies.
  • > Epstein later coined the term "generative social science" — if you haven't grown it from simple rules, you haven't fully explained it.
  • > Natural selection in Sugarscape favors high-vision, low-metabolism agents. Over time, the surviving population's average traits shift — evolution without any genetic algorithm.
