# Organizational Inertia and Mental Model Rigidity

## Sources
- Gilbert, "Unbundling the Structure of Inertia," Academy of Management Review, 2005
- Kaplan & Henderson, "Inertia and Incentives: Bridging Organizational Economics and Organizational Theory," Organization Science, 2005
- Tripsas & Gavetti, "Capabilities, Cognition, and Inertia," Strategic Management Journal, 2000

## Types of Organizational Inertia (Gilbert 2005)

1. **Resource rigidity**: Failure to change resource investment patterns in response to discontinuous change
2. **Routine rigidity**: Failure to change organizational processes that use resources
3. **Cognitive rigidity**: Inability to update mental models of how the world works

## Psychological Mechanisms

- **Cognitive consistency**: Holding contradictory ideas creates dissonance; easier to ignore new information
- **Confirmation bias**: Seeking evidence that confirms existing mental models
- **Loss aversion** (Kahneman): Losses loom larger than gains, making change feel risky
- **Competency traps**: Success with current approach reinforces commitment to it

## Three-Part Inertia Model

1. **Insight inertia**: Related to mental models and action theories
2. **Action inertia**: From management assumptions and control mechanisms
3. **Psychological inertia**: From stress, anxiety, and defensive responses to change

## Connection to Rigids vs Flexibles

Rigid agents embody organizational inertia:
- They represent deeply embedded cognitive frames
- Their experience bonus represents the competency trap
- Their inability to switch strategies represents mental model rigidity
- The promotion of rigids creates organizational-level inertia through structural selection
- Even when the environment clearly changes, the rigid-dominated hierarchy resists adaptation

Flexible agents represent the capacity to overcome inertia:
- They observe the environment and update their approach
- But they pay a cost: noise in observation, lower experience accumulation
- They represent "organizational slack" or adaptive capacity
- Having too few flexibles = organization can't adapt when it needs to
- Having too many = organization never develops deep competencies
