# Ambidextrous Organizations (Tushman & O'Reilly)

## Sources
- Tushman & O'Reilly, "Ambidextrous Organizations: Managing Evolutionary and Revolutionary Change," California Management Review, 1996
- O'Reilly & Tushman, "The Ambidextrous Organization," Harvard Business Review, 2004

## Core Concept

Organizations must simultaneously:
- **Exploit**: Optimize existing products, processes, and competencies (incremental innovation)
- **Explore**: Experiment with new ideas, markets, and technologies (discontinuous innovation)

## Organizational Design Solution

Ambidextrous organizations separate exploratory units from exploitative ones:
- Different processes, structures, and cultures for each
- Tight integration at senior executive level
- Allows coexistence of fundamentally different operational modes

## Empirical Results

In examination of 35 breakthrough innovation attempts:
- **90%+ success** with ambidextrous structure
- **0% success** with cross-functional or unsupported teams
- **25% success** with functional designs

## Connection to Rigids vs Flexibles

The ambidextrous organization concept maps to having a mix of rigid and flexible agents:
- Rigid agents = exploitation units (efficient, experienced, but brittle)
- Flexible agents = exploration units (adaptive, but less efficient)
- The key question: what's the optimal ratio?
- Senior leadership integration = how composition at top of hierarchy affects whole org
- If top levels are all rigid, the org loses ability to explore
- Some flexible presence at all levels provides adaptation capacity

## Simulation Implication

The sweep mode should explore how varying the flexible "reserve" percentage affects:
- Steady-state performance (flexibles are a drag during stability)
- Transition costs (flexibles accelerate adaptation after regime change)
- Overall long-run performance (the optimal reserve is non-zero)
